# 🔒 IT Security: Defense against the digital dark arts

## 📝 Summary
This course focuses on developing skills in key IT security domains, including security management, malware protection, system hardening, and threat detection and management. It also covers foundational areas like network security, data security, cryptography, and IT security architecture.

## 💡 Skills and Competencies Gained
| Module Focus | Key Skills & Knowledge Acquired |
|--------------|---------------------------------|
| Understanding Security Threats | This module established security's foundational language, framing threats through the lens of the `CIA Triad`. It systematically categorized risks, from malware and network-based assaults like `DDoS` to deceptive social engineering, providing a structured vocabulary for analyzing how each attack uniquely compromises confidentiality, integrity, or availability. This shifted the perspective from a vague sense of "being secure" to a precise understanding of specific adversary tactics and their intended outcomes. |
| Pelcgbybtl (Cryptology) | The module demystified cryptography by framing it as a practical toolkit rather than an abstract concept, where the key revelation was understanding how different tools solve specific problems in tandem. Symmetric encryption (like `AES`) is the workhorse for efficiently securing bulk data, but its weakness safely sharing the key is solved by asymmetric encryption (like `RSA`), which uses a public-private key pair to enable secure key exchange without ever revealing the private key. This foundation is crowned by hashing (like `SHA-256`), which acts as a tamper-proof seal to verify data integrity, and the entire system is held together by Public Key Infrastructure (`PKI`), which uses digitally signed certificates from trusted authorities to answer the critical question of identity, ensuring that a public key actually belongs to who it claims to this chain of trust is what silently secures every `HTTPS` connection and makes modern digital communication possible. |
| The 3 A's of Cybersecurity: Authentication, Authorization, Accounting | It detailed the `AAA` framework as the core of access management. It moved beyond simple passwords to the necessity of multifactor authentication (`MFA`) and explored robust protocols like `Kerberos` and `RADIUS` for centralized network access control. The concepts of authorization defined permissions through models like `DAC` and `MAC`, while accounting emphasized the critical practice of logging access for auditing and security analysis, forming a complete cycle for verifying identity, enforcing policy, and tracking activity. |
| Securing Your Networks | Focused on proactive network defense, emphasizing hardening techniques for both hardware and software to reduce attack surfaces. It critically analyzed wireless security evolution, explicitly detailing the cryptographic flaws that rendered `WEP` obsolete and advocating for `WPA2/WPA3`. The curriculum also introduced active monitoring strategies using tools like `Wireshark` and `tcpdump` for traffic analysis, and outlined the role of Intrusion Detection/Prevention Systems (`IDS/IPS`) in identifying and mitigating malicious activity in real-time.|
| Denfense in Depth | Detailed the principle of defense-in-depth at the host level, layering security controls to protect individual systems. Key strategies included hardening the `OS` by disabling non-essential services, deploying host-based firewalls for granular traffic control, and implementing robust antimalware solutions. It also stressed the importance of `full-disk encryption` for data-at-rest security, a disciplined software patch management cycle to eliminate vulnerabilities, and comprehensive logging and auditing to enable detection and forensic analysis of security incidents.|
| Creating a Company Culture for Security | Shifted the focus from purely technical controls to the human and organizational elements of security. It emphasized that technology is only one layer of defense. Key topics included: <li>**Risk Assessment**: The foundational practice of systematically identifying and prioritizing potential threats to the organization.</li> <li>**The Human Firewall**: The critical role of continuous security training and fostering secure user habits to prevent social engineering and mistakes.</li> <li>**Third-Party Risk**: The importance of vetting vendors and managing third-party security, as your defenses are only as strong as your partners' weakest link.</li> <li>**Incident Response**: Having a clear plan for incident reporting, analysis, and recovery to handle breaches effectively when they occur.</li> <li>**Policies and Culture**: Establishing strong privacy policies and a "Bring Your Own Device" (BYOD) strategy to create a security-conscious culture from the top down.</li> |
